### It's not just about nice pictures and videos

So far, we have looked at video, image, and audio achievements of AI. However, the list is obviously not complete without arguably the most popular application today – AI chatbots.

The scale of AI chatbots is difficult to overstate. These tools have evolved from curiosities to everyday companions. They can answer questions in seconds, draft messages effortlessly, and brainstorm ideas in real time like never before. The growth was something never seen before – ChatGPT surpassed 1 million users in just 5 days after launching. Right now, it has more than 4 billion visits per month, while users send more than 2 billion messages each day. 

The real breakthrough, then, has been in language. Why? Because language is how humans store and share knowledge. Mastering language is like mastering thought itself.

Large Language Models (LLMs) (the technology behind AI chatbots) do not “think” in the same way humans do, but they work in a surprisingly brain-like way. Just as our neurons fire in patterns when we predict what comes next in a sentence, LLMs predict the next word by activating patterns learned from billions of examples. They do not need explicit rules for grammar or logic – just exposure to enough text.

The strange part is that humanity may have built intelligence or something very close to it without fully understanding it. Human brains also work by prediction, even if it is not yet identified how thought emerges. 

<br>

### Why didn’t LLMs exist in 1990? Two main reasons.

* **1. Data:** In 1990, the web was just starting. There wasn’t enough text online to teach a system the patterns of language and the facts of the world. Over the last 10–15 years, the explosion of blogs, Wikipedia, forums, research papers, and social media has finally provided the scale of data needed. The web became humanity’s library.

* **2. Compute:** Even if humanity had the data back then, computers weren’t powerful enough to process it. Training a large model means running trillions of calculations. In 1990, that would’ve taken decades. The turning point came with GPUs (graphics processing units), chips originally built to render video game graphics, but also very good at doing many math operations in parallel. That parallelism turned out to be exactly what training LLMs requires. Once Nvidia and others scaled up GPU clusters, suddenly it was possible to crunch all that internet data. (Which is also why Nvidia has since become the most valuable company in the world.)

Put simply, the web provided loads of data in human language format, and GPUs provided the capacity to train a model on this data.

# ChatGPT platform overview

The ChatGPT Platform by OpenAI is quite a universal workspace. Everything happens in one environment, allowing you to move easily between chatting and creating custom assistants. It's powered by a type of artificial intelligence known as a GPT, short for Generative Pre-trained Transformer. You can think of it as an advanced language model that understands your words and generates natural, human-like replies:
- **Generative:** It creates new content (like text, summaries, emails, or code) from scratch.
- **Pre-trained:** It’s trained on massive amounts of publicly available internet data and documents, so it already knows how humans write.
- **Transformer:** A deep learning architecture designed to understand the relationships between words and ideas in context.

What is worth mentioning is that you can also integrate it with various tools like Python or calendar apps to automate different tasks. Check some use cases in this [quick tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mHOLzwe4sMQ).

## Is AI a bubble?

In recent months, the debate around whether AI is a “bubble” has become louder. Even `Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI`, [has publicly warned that the hype cycle could resemble past bubbles](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/08/18/openai-sam-altman-warns-ai-market-is-in-a-bubble.html). The problem is that this statement is now often repeated without context: people throw around the word “bubble” as if it were self-explanatory, without asking what kind of bubble we are talking about or what history teaches us. The result is a lot of noise, with everyone saying “bubble, bubble, bubble” without much substance.

<br>

### What is a bubble?

A `bubble` is a situation where excitement and speculation push prices or attention far beyond real, lasting value. When the bubble bursts, prices fall and many lose out. The word is often used negatively, linked to financial crashes like 2007–08, but some bubbles also leave behind useful technologies or infrastructure that continue to benefit society. Let's distinguish between two types of bubbles:  

* `Speculative & filter bubbles` (e.g., the housing crisis of 2007–08): driven mainly by short-term profit and herd behaviour. When they burst, they leave behind financial losses but little lasting value. You can find more insights about the filter bubbles in [this article](https://fs.blog/filter-bubbles/).

* `Technological bubbles` (e.g., railroads, cars, planes, personal computers, the web): even though they also attract speculation and crashes, they finance real innovation. When the dust settles, the underlying technology remains and reshapes society.

The challenge is that in technology markets, these two bubbles often overlap. Speculators looking for quick returns end up funding innovators, so the line between destructive hype and productive investment is blurry.

---

## Delusional technologies from the near past

Over the past decade, several technologies have captured public and media attention with bold promises of revolutionising industries. However, many have since seen slower-than-expected adoption, limited impact, or a failure to deliver on their most ambitious claims.

- **3D printing:** It was initially hyped as a revolutionary technology for manufacturing and prototyping. While it has made significant strides and found niche applications, widespread adoption and its transformative impact are limited to a few industries.

- **The Metaverse:** Popularised by Meta’s rebrand in 2020, the metaverse was hyped as the future of work, play, and social life in immersive virtual spaces. Despite heavy investment, it failed to gain traction due to high costs, a clunky user experience, and a lack of compelling use cases.

- **Blockchain, Web3, and NFTs:** Blockchain was heavily hyped as a replacement for traditional currencies like the US Dollar or Euro, but most cryptocurrency activity has centred on speculation or, in some cases, illegal transactions. Broader applications are still being tested but remain limited in scale. Web3, built on the same technology, envisions a decentralised internet where users own their data and assets, yet adoption has stayed low due to technical complexity and unclear real-world value. NFTs became the most visible expression of this wave in 2022, briefly exploding as digital collectibles and art before collapsing in value and leaving behind widespread scepticism about their long-term utility.

> [This article](https://www.visualcapitalist.com/technology-hype-cycles-2000-2018/) shows how different technology hype cycles have played out over the past decade.

---

## But are bubbles bad or good for humanity?

Economic history suggests that while bubbles can cause short-term pain, they often leave behind assets, technologies, or institutions that continue to shape society long after the speculation ends. This **spillover effect** is what makes many technological bubbles ultimately transformative:  

* **Railroads (19th century):** The rapid expansion created a financial bubble – by 1894, a quarter of the US rail system was bankrupt. Yet the physical tracks remained, enabling cheaper and more reliable transportation, which transformed economic life.

* **Computers (mid-20th century):** Early computers were expensive, slow, and dismissed by many as impractical for everyday use. Yet massive government and corporate investment pushed the technology forward, laying the groundwork for the personal computing revolution that reshaped work and communication.

* **Dot-com boom (1990s):** Billions of dollars flowed into internet startups, many of which quickly went bankrupt after the 2000 crash. Still, the infrastructure, talent, and surviving firms (like Amazon and Google) became the backbone of today’s digital economy.

Bubbles may be disruptive when they burst, but their long-term legacy often redirects the trajectory of technology and society for the better.

<br>

Another feature of bubbles is **Fear Of Missing Out (FOMO)**. Often criticised as herd mentality, FOMO can also be a powerful driver of innovation. Besides speculators, it attracts people at every stage of their careers, each responding in different ways:  

* `Entrepreneurs` use the flood of cheap capital to scale ideas quickly. *Jeff Bezos, for instance, rode the dot-com wave to transform Amazon from a small bookstore into a global marketplace.*  

* `Young talent` joins early, challenging the status quo and shaping new industries. Some push for critical changes (such as the legal protections that enabled web startups in the 1990s), while others go on to found companies themselves, as with the so-called *PayPal Mafia*.  

* `Experienced executives` who may be more risk-averse but bring valuable experience often move from corporate roles into startups. *For example, Eric Schmidt left a traditional career path to join Google as CEO in 2001, bringing management expertise that helped transform it from a scrappy startup into a dominant tech giant.* 

Bubbles may burst, but FOMO ensures they pull in people with bold ideas, restless energy, and hard-earned experience. This is often the exact mix that allows breakthrough technologies to survive the crash.

## How do bubbles burst?

In the `dot-com bubble`, many startups spent heavily on marketing to chase growth, but underinvested in building sustainable products and R&D. When profits failed to appear, investor confidence collapsed and funding dried up.

In the `2007–2008 financial crisis`, housing prices stopped rising. As values fell, homeowners with risky loans could no longer refinance, defaults surged, and the collapse spread through the financial system.

<br>

### Why do we have bubbles? So why not pursue safe, incremental progress?

`Incremental progress` is a synonym for a world where no one gets too excited about the possibilities of the future, because no one is excited about the future at all. It’s depressing and leads to conflict. A static economy (without bubbles) leads to a zero-sum game and recession. Disputes will happen over who should be a loser. People start to anticipate a worsening future. Under such circumstances, ambitious people will be attracted to zero-sum games: adversarial politics, speculation, fraud, revolutions, and coups.

From a psychological perspective, desire is often shaped not by individual preference but by what is perceived as desirable to others, a concept known as `mimetic desire theory`, popularised by René Girard. This dynamic creates cycles of imitation and competition that can escalate into conflict. In this light, bubbles can be seen as a form of escape from the broader mimetic trap: they redirect potentially destructive dynamics towards ambitious goals, channelling collective energy into the pursuit of innovations that had not yet been achieved.

### So, is AI a bubble?

Bubbles are impossible to diagnose in real-time. Only in retrospect do we know whether exuberance was justified or delusional.

Think of this as flying a plane. Pilots don’t rely on a single instrument. They monitor a cluster of gauges to understand how the flight is going:

For now, it looks like we may not be in a bubble or at least a dangerous stage of it, but this could change fast. We are definitely in an AI boom, though. What matters is that if the technology proves genuinely helpful, something enduring will come out of the current wave.

If AI does turn into a bubble and eventually bursts, it is worth remembering that some of the most life-changing technologies emerged from such cycles, like personal computers and the internet. Unlike Blockchain, Web3, or NFTs – technologies that remained niche or speculative – AI is not confined to a small corner of the economy. Its applications span healthcare, education, business, science, and everyday tools. That scalability is what could make AI a true revolution rather than just another temporary hype cycle.

## Review of the current AI terminology

In this section, you will explore further some of the most commonly used terms connected to AI.

<br>

<details>

<summary>Generative AI</summary>

* `What it is:` Generative AI refers to artificial intelligence systems that can **create new, original content** like text, images, audio, video, or even code, rather than just analyzing or classifying existing data.

* `Why it's important:` Traditional AI often focuses on *understanding* or *predicting*. Generative AI takes it a step further by *producing*. This capability opens up vast possibilities for content creation, design, and personalized experiences, transforming industries from art to marketing.

* `Analogy:` If traditional AI is like an art critic who can analyze paintings, Generative AI is like an artist who can paint new, unique masterpieces.

</details>

<br>

<details>

<summary>Large Language Models (LLMs)</summary>

* `What it is:` LLMs are a specific type of **Generative AI** primarily designed to understand, generate, and process human language. They are "large" because they are trained on immense datasets of text and code (billions or trillions of words) and have billions of parameters, allowing them to learn complex patterns in language.

* `Why it's important:` Their vast training enables them to perform a wide range of language-based tasks, from writing essays and answering questions to summarizing documents and translating languages, with remarkable fluency and coherence. They are the technology behind tools like ChatGPT.

* `Analogy:` Imagine a super-librarian who has read every book ever written and can not only answer any question but also write new books, poems, or essays in any style you ask for.

</details>

<br>

<details>

<summary>Multimodal AI</summary>

* `What it is:` Multimodal AI refers to AI systems that can **process and understand information from multiple types of data inputs simultaneously**. This means they can take in and make sense of text, images, audio, video, and other data sources together.

* `Why it's important:` The real world isn't just text or just images, but it's a rich mix. Multimodal AI allows for a more holistic understanding, leading to more human-like interactions and capabilities, such as describing a video's content, answering questions about an image, or generating a video from text and sound cues.

* `Analogy:` Imagine a skilled detective who not only reads witness statements (text) but also analyzes surveillance footage (video), listens to recordings (audio), and examines crime scene photos (images) – combining all this information for a complete picture.

</details>

<br>

<details>

<summary>Tokens</summary>

* `What it is:` A token is the basic unit of text representation in LLMs, as defined by a tokenizer. The tokenizer is a tool that breaks down raw text into these smaller pieces so the model can process it. A token isn’t always a whole word: it could be part of a word, a whole word, a punctuation mark, or even a space. For example, the word “unbelievable” might be split into “un”, “believ”, and “able”.

* `Why it's important:` AIs don't understand words like humans do. They convert text into these numerical tokens to process them. Understanding tokens is crucial for:
    * **Context Window Limits:** AI models have a fixed "context window" which is measured in tokens (e.g., 8K, 32K, 128K tokens). This limit dictates how much text (your prompt plus the AI's generated response) the AI can "remember" or process at one time.
    * **Cost Calculation:** AI usage is often priced per token.

* `Analogy:` If human language is like a string of beads, tokens are the individual beads: sometimes small parts of words, sometimes whole words, sometimes just punctuation. The tokenizer is like the tool that cuts the string into beads in the first place, deciding where one bead ends and the next begins. The AI then sees and processes these beads one by one.

<br>

</details>

<br>

<details>

<summary>Context Window</summary>

- `What it is:` The context window is the maximum number of tokens (units of text) a language model can process at once. It includes both the user’s input and the model’s output. Anything beyond this limit cannot be “remembered” or used by the model.

- `Why it's important:` A larger context window allows the model to handle longer texts, multiple documents, and even different modalities (text, images, audio). Modern models can process tens of thousands of words, enabling use cases like analyzing entire books, exploring large datasets, or managing complex conversations. However, the limit still exists, so strategies like summarization or chunking text are often required.

- `Analogy:` Think of it like short-term memory: with a small memory, you can only keep track of a few sentences. With a large memory, you can recall entire chapters without losing the thread.

</details>
